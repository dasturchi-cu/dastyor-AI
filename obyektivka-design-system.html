How systematic land appropriation reshaped three rural districts over a decade

A 14-month investigation tracking 847 land transactions reveals a coordinated pattern of displacement affecting over 12,000 families across the Fergana Valley.

AT
Aziz Toshmatov, Nilufar Rashidova
March 9, 2024 · 24 min read · Investigations

Fergana Valley, aerial survey 2023. Photo: Obyektivka Archive

In the spring of 2013, Hamid Nazarov received a notice that the land his family had farmed for three generations would be reclassified under a new agricultural modernization directive. He had 60 days to vacate. His neighbors received similar letters within the week.

What followed over the next decade was not, as officials maintained, a series of disconnected administrative decisions. Our investigation — which analyzed 847 land transactions, reviewed 12,000 pages of government documents, and interviewed 340 affected families — reveals a coordinated, methodical process of displacement.

"We were not displaced by a policy. We were displaced by a decision that someone, somewhere, had already made. The paperwork came after."

The paper trail

Every land transaction in the three affected districts follows an identical procedural sequence. First, a technical inspection classifies land as "unproductive." Then, within a median of 34 days, a state enterprise submits a development application. The application is approved in under two weeks in 91% of documented cases.

We identified 12 senior officials whose signatures appear across the majority of these transactions. All 12 were promoted or received significant budget increases within 18 months of the transactions they approved.

Methodology

This investigation used a combination of public records, leaked internal documents verified by three independent sources, satellite imagery analysis conducted with a European geospatial research institute, and on-the-ground reporting across 14 villages.
